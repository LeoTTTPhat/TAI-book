\chapter{The AI Trust Threat Matrix: A Structured Gap-Analysis
         Framework}
\label{ch:threat-matrix}

\begin{quote}
\itshape
A security posture is only as strong as the attack it has never
considered.  The Trustworthy AI Architect does not wait for an
adversary to discover a gap; they map the gaps themselves, first.
\end{quote}

\abstract{%
Chapters~6, 11, 17, and 23 each introduced specific attack
techniques against AI systems---Membership Inference, adversarial
evasion, prompt injection, and autonomous escalation respectively.
What those chapters did not provide is a unified, structured
framework that an Architect can use to perform a \emph{gap analysis}:
a systematic comparison of the attack surface of their deployed
system against the full catalogue of known techniques.  This chapter
introduces the \textbf{AI Trust Threat Matrix (ATTM)}, modelled on
the structure of MITRE ATT\&CK~\cite{mitre2023} and the OWASP Top~10
for LLMs~\cite{owasp-llm-2023}, but extended to cover the complete
Trustworthy AI Stack defined in Table~1.3.  The ATTM organises 28
attack techniques into 6 tactical categories, maps each technique to
its primary architectural defence in this book, and provides a
machine-readable Gap Analyser that scores a deployment's coverage.%
}

% ------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Why a Unified Threat Matrix?}
\label{sec:attm-rationale}
% ------------------------------------------------------------------

MITRE ATT\&CK~\cite{mitre2023} demonstrated that cataloguing adversary
behaviour in a structured, tactic--technique hierarchy produces three
concrete benefits that ad-hoc threat modelling does not: it enforces
completeness (no tactic category is accidentally omitted), it enables
comparison across organisations (a shared vocabulary for ``what we
defend against''), and it produces actionable gap scores (a technique
with no mapped defence is an unambiguous finding, not an opinion).

The AI security landscape has lacked an equivalent.  Existing frameworks
address subsets of the problem:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{MITRE ATLAS}~\cite{mitre-atlas-2023} catalogues
        adversarial ML techniques but does not map them to the privacy
        and verifiability pillars of the Trustworthy AI Stack.
  \item \textbf{OWASP LLM Top~10}~\cite{owasp-llm-2023} covers
        generative AI application risks but omits distributed-system
        threats (Byzantine attacks, gradient leakage) and
        post-quantum concerns.
  \item \textbf{NIST AI RMF}~\cite{nist-ai-rmf-2026} provides a risk
        management vocabulary but does not map risks to specific
        cryptographic or statistical defences.
\end{itemize}

The ATTM synthesises these sources and extends them across all four
pillars of the 2026 Trustworthy AI Stack
(Table~\ref{tab:trustworthy-ai-stack}): Security, Privacy,
Verifiability, and Alignment.  It is the operational complement to
the economic RAV Framework of Appendix~A: where RAV answers
``what is it worth to defend?'', the ATTM answers
``what are we defending against, and are we actually doing it?''

\section{Tactic Taxonomy}
\label{sec:attm-taxonomy}

adversary's high-level goal---following the ATT\&CK convention of
separating \emph{what the adversary wants} from \emph{how they achieve

Stack.

\section{Performing a Gap Analysis}
\label{sec:attm-gap-analysis}
% ------------------------------------------------------------------

The ATTM is operationalised through a structured Gap Analysis
workflow.  The workflow produces a \textbf{Coverage Score} and a
\textbf{Priority Remediation List} that the Architect can present
alongside the RAV calculations of Appendix~A.

\subsubsection*{Step 1: Scope the Deployment}

Not every technique in the ATTM is applicable to every deployment.
The Architect first filters the matrix to the \emph{in-scope technique
set} $\mathcal{T}_{\text{scope}}$ based on two criteria:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Deployment modality.}  A deployment using only batch
        inference with no federated components may exclude T2 techniques
        (ATT-06 through ATT-08) that require a multi-client federated
        setting.  A non-generative deployment may exclude ATT-01
        (Training Data Extraction) and ATT-02 (Prompt Leakage).

  \item \textbf{Risk tier.}  For Low-risk deployments under Vietnam's
        AI Law, techniques rated \medium\ may be deferred to a future
        review cycle.  For High-risk and Critical deployments, all
        in-scope techniques regardless of severity must be addressed.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection*{Step 2: Score Each Technique}

For each $t \in \mathcal{T}_{\text{scope}}$, the Architect assigns a
Gap Flag from three states:

\begin{description}
  \item[\checkmark\ (Deployed).] The primary defence is implemented,
        tested against the ATTM acceptance criteria for that technique,
        and monitored in production via the Trustworthy MLOps pipeline
        (Chapter~25).
  \item[? (Partial).] The defence is partially implemented or is
        present in staging but not production, or has not been tested
        against the specific technique.
  \item[\ding{55}\ (Gap).] No defence addresses this technique.
        Immediate remediation required for Critical and High severity;
        deferred remediation permitted for Medium severity.
\end{description}

\subsubsection*{Step 3: Compute the Coverage Score}

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:attm-coverage-score}
\text{Coverage}(\mathcal{T}_{\text{scope}}) =
\frac{\sum_{t \in \mathcal{T}_{\text{scope}}}
      s_t \cdot w_t}
     {\sum_{t \in \mathcal{T}_{\text{scope}}} w_t}
\end{equation}

where $s_t \in \{1.0, 0.5, 0.0\}$ for \checkmark, ?, \ding{55}\
respectively, and $w_t$ is the severity weight:
$w_t = 3$ (\critical), $w_t = 2$ (\high), $w_t = 1$ (\medium).

A Coverage Score below 0.85 for a High-Risk Vietnamese deployment
should be treated as a Conformity Assessment risk: regulators may
require evidence of gap remediation before approving production use.

\subsubsection*{Step 4: Prioritise Remediation}

Gaps are prioritised by the product of severity weight and RAV Ratio
from Table~A.3.  A \critical\ gap in ATT-01 (Training Data Extraction)
with a RAV Ratio of 36.9 (Table~A.4) receives priority score
$3 \times 36.9 = 110.7$; a \medium\ gap in ATT-20 (Gaze Attack) with
a lower RAV Ratio receives a commensurately lower priority.  The
Architect addresses gaps in descending priority order subject to the
carbon budget constraints of Section~\ref{sec:trust-sustainability}.

\paragraph{Step 3: Coverage Score.}

\begin{equation*}
\text{Coverage} =
\frac{(3 \times 1.0) + (3 \times 0.0) + (3 \times 0.5) +
      (3 \times 1.0) + (3 \times 0.0) + (3 \times 0.5) +
      (3 \times 1.0) + (3 \times 0.0) + \ldots_{\text{13 remaining}}}
     {21 \times \bar{w}}
\approx 0.61
\end{equation*}

# ----------------------------------------------------------------
# Gap Analyser
# ----------------------------------------------------------------

class ATTMGapAnalyser:
    """
    Scores a deployment's coverage against the ATTM and produces
    a prioritised remediation report.
    """

    def __init__(self,
                 deployment_modality: dict,
                 risk_tier: str = "HIGH"):
        """
        deployment_modality: dict with boolean keys:
          federated, multimodal, metaverse, inter_agent
        risk_tier: "LOW" | "MEDIUM" | "HIGH" | "CRITICAL"
        """
        self.modality  = deployment_modality
        self.risk_tier = risk_tier
        self._techniques = self._scope_techniques()

    def _scope_techniques(self) -> List[Technique]:
        """Filter catalogue to in-scope techniques."""
        result = []
        for t in ATTM_CATALOGUE:
            if t.requires_federated  and not self.modality.get("federated"):
                continue
            if t.requires_multimodal and not self.modality.get("multimodal"):
                continue
            if t.requires_metaverse  and not self.modality.get("metaverse"):
                continue
            if t.requires_interagent and not self.modality.get("inter_agent"):
                continue
            # For LOW risk tier, defer MEDIUM severity
            if self.risk_tier == "LOW" \
                    and t.severity == Severity.MEDIUM:
                continue
            result.append(t)
        return result

    def record_assessment(self, tech_id: str,
                          flag: GapFlag,
                          evidence: str = ""):
        """Record a Gap Flag for a specific technique."""
        for t in self._techniques:
            if t.id == tech_id:
                t.gap_flag = flag
                t.evidence = evidence
                return
        raise ValueError(f"Technique {tech_id} not in scope.")

    def coverage_score(self) -> float:
        """Compute weighted Coverage Score (Eq. 24.attm.1)."""
        assessed = [t for t in self._techniques
                    if t.gap_flag is not None]
        if not assessed:
            return 0.0
        num = sum(t.gap_flag.value * t.severity.value
                  for t in assessed)
        den = sum(t.severity.value for t in assessed)
        return round(num / den, 4) if den else 0.0

    def gap_report(self) -> dict:
        """
        Generate structured gap report with prioritised
        remediation list sorted by (severity * rav_proxy).
        """
        gaps = [t for t in self._techniques
                if t.gap_flag in (GapFlag.GAP, GapFlag.PARTIAL,
                                  None)]
        # Priority = severity_weight * 2 (proxy for avg RAV ratio)
        gaps_sorted = sorted(
            gaps,
            key=lambda t: t.severity.value * 2,
            reverse=True)

        return {
            "deployment_risk_tier":  self.risk_tier,
            "scoped_technique_count":len(self._techniques),
            "assessed_count":        sum(1 for t in self._techniques
                                         if t.gap_flag is not None),
            "coverage_score":        self.coverage_score(),
            "conformity_threshold":  0.85,
            "meets_threshold":       self.coverage_score() >= 0.85,
            "gaps": [
                {
                    "id":              t.id,
                    "name":            t.name,
                    "tactic":          t.tactic.value,
                    "severity":        t.severity.name,
                    "status":          t.gap_flag.name
                                       if t.gap_flag else "UNASSESSED",
                    "primary_defence": t.primary_defence,
                    "evidence":        t.evidence or "",
                }
                for t in gaps_sorted
            ],
        }

    def export_json(self, path: str):
        """Export full assessment to JSON for MLOps dashboard."""
        payload = self.gap_report()
        payload["full_assessment"] = [
            {
                "id":       t.id,
                "name":     t.name,
                "severity": t.severity.name,
                "flag":     t.gap_flag.name if t.gap_flag else "UNASSESSED",
                "evidence": t.evidence or "",
            }
            for t in self._techniques
        ]
        with open(path, "w") as f:
            json.dump(payload, f, indent=2)
        return path

\section{Conclusion}
% ------------------------------------------------------------------

The AI Trust Threat Matrix transforms the book's individual defence
mechanisms from a catalogue into a \emph{posture}: a structured,
measurable, improvable configuration of protections against a known
adversary landscape.  The Gap Analyser of
Listing~\ref{lst:attm-gap-analyser} makes this posture machine-readable,
enabling integration into the Trustworthy MLOps pipeline of Chapter~25
as a continuous coverage metric alongside the Trust Debt Score of
Equation~\ref{eq:trust-debt-score}.

A deployment whose Gap Analysis produces a Coverage Score below 0.85
is not merely ``less secure.''  Under Vietnam's AI Law and the EU AI
Act, it is a deployment whose Conformity Assessment documentation
contains a quantified, unresolved risk---a liability that the RAV
Framework of Appendix~A can translate directly into expected financial
exposure.  The ATTM ensures that no Architect is surprised by that
liability on the day of audit.
