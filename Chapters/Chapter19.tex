\chapter{Machine Unlearning: The Right to be Forgotten}
\label{chap:machine_unlearning}

\abstract{As global regulations like the GDPR and Vietnam's Decree 13/2023 grant users the "Right to be Forgotten," the Trustworthy AI Architect must go beyond simple database deletions. This chapter introduces the emerging field of Machine Unlearning---technical methods for removing the influence of specific training records from already-deployed models. We analyze the differences between exact retraining and approximate unlearning using influence functions and the Hessian matrix. We also explore the unique challenges of unlearning in distributed systems and Large Language Models, where "forgetting" a sample must be balanced against maintaining overall model stability.}

\section{When Users Ask to be Forgotten}
\label{sec:unlearning_intro_ch17}

In a world governed by privacy regulations, removing a user's raw data from a database is often legally insufficient if that data's "signature" remains embedded within a trained machine learning model. If a model can still produce an output that depends specifically on a deleted record, the user has not truly been forgotten.

\textbf{Machine Unlearning} is the process of updating a model such that its weights reflect what they would have been if the "forgotten" data point had never existed, without the prohibitive cost of retraining the entire model from scratch.

\section{Core Unlearning Methodologies}
\label{sec:unlearning_methods_ch17}

The choice of unlearning method depends on the required strength of the guarantee and the computational budget:

\begin{description}
  \item[Exact Unlearning (Retraining):] 
        The perfectly compliant approach. The model is retrained on the dataset $D \setminus D_f$. While providing a perfect guarantee, it is $O(N)$ and becomes computationally prohibitive for Large Language Models or massive industrial datasets.
  \item[Approximate Unlearning (Influence Functions):]
        For many deep learning models, we can estimate the \textit{influence} of a specific data point $D_f$ on the model parameters $\theta$ and update the weights inversely. This is often calculated using the inverse of the Hessian matrix:
        \begin{equation}
        \theta_{\text{new}} = \theta_{\text{old}} + \epsilon \cdot H^{-1} \sum_{x \in D_f} \nabla_\theta L(\theta_{\text{old}}, x)
        \end{equation}
\end{description}

\section{Unlearning in Distributed Systems}
\label{sec:federated_unlearning_ch17}

In Federated Learning environments, unlearning becomes even more complex. The server must remove a specific client's contribution without ever having had access to their raw data. This is typically achieved through \textbf{Federated Unlearning} protocols, where the aggregator subtracts a normalized version of the client's historical updates and performs a small number of "repair" rounds to re-stabilize the global model.

\section{Implementation: Gradient Ascent}
\label{sec:gradient_ascent_ch17}

A simple, albeit potentially unstable, implementation of unlearning involves "undoing" the training step through gradient ascent on the forget set. By maximizing the loss on the data points we wish to forget, we move the model weights away from those specific patterns. However, to prevent the model from collapsing, this must be combined with regularization that keeps the weights close to their original values for the remainder of the dataset.

\section{Unlearning Verification: Proof of Forgetting}
\label{sec:unlearning_verification}
% ------------------------------------------------------------------

The technical challenge of machine unlearning has a parallel legal challenge: how does an architect \emph{prove} to a regulator or data subject that the forgotten data no longer influences the model? Under Decree 13 Article 8 and GDPR Article 17, the burden of proof lies with the data controller. A verbal assertion that ``the model was retrained'' is insufficient; the architect must provide an auditable, quantitative \textbf{Unlearning Verification}.

\subsection{Membership Inference as a Verification Unit Test}
\label{subsec:mia_verification}

The most principled verification method reuses the \textbf{Membership Inference Attack (MIA)} framework (Chapter~\ref{chap:privacy_attacks}) as a canary test against the forget set $D_f$:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Baseline MIA AUC (pre-unlearning):} Run a shadow-model MIA targeting $D_f$ against the original model. A typical trained model yields AUC $\approx 0.70$--$0.85$ for memorized samples.
  \item \textbf{Post-unlearning MIA AUC target:} After applying the chosen unlearning method, re-run the same MIA. Successful unlearning must reduce the forget-set AUC to the \textbf{random-guess band}: \textbf{AUC $\leq 0.52$}.
  \item \textbf{Control MIA AUC on $D_{\mathrm{retain}}$:} Confirm the retain-set MIA AUC is unchanged from the pre-unlearning baseline ($\pm 0.02$). A significant drop signals catastrophic forgetting rather than targeted unlearning.
\end{enumerate}

The three-way test produces a \textbf{Proof of Forgetting Certificate}: a signed report containing forget-set AUC, retain-set AUC, and test configuration, suitable for submission to a regulatory authority as documented evidence of erasure.

\section{Decision Tree for Unlearning Requests}
\label{sec:unlearning_decision_tree}
% ------------------------------------------------------------------

The choice between Exact and Approximate Unlearning is not merely a technical question---it is a risk-calibrated architectural decision that depends on the sensitivity of the data being forgotten, the current Trust Debt of the model, and whether the system was designed with SISA partitioning. This section provides a structured \textbf{Decision Tree for Unlearning Requests}.

\subsection{The Four Decision Dimensions}
\label{sec:unlearning_dimensions}

\begin{description}
  \item[Dimension 1: Data Sensitivity Class.]
    Classify the data to be forgotten using the sensitivity taxonomy established in Chapter~\ref{chap:privacy_attacks}: \textit{Class A} (biometric, health, political affiliation --- highest), \textit{Class B} (financial records, location history --- high), \textit{Class C} (behavioral preferences, purchase history --- moderate), \textit{Class D} (aggregated public data --- low). Class A or B demands the strongest guarantee.

  \item[Dimension 2: SISA Architecture Availability.]
    If the model was originally trained with \textbf{SISA (Sharded, Isolated, Sliced, and Aggregated)} decomposition, only the affected shard(s) need retraining---a $1/S$ fraction of the full retraining cost where $S$ is the number of shards. If SISA was not used in training, only Exact or Approximate methods apply.

  \item[Dimension 3: Trust Debt Score.]
    A model with a high Trust Debt Score (Chapter~\ref{chap:attm})---many ATTM gaps, weak DP guarantees, no formal verification---is one where Approximate Unlearning poses elevated residual risk. The forgotten sample's influence may not be fully neutralized by gradient-based methods if the model was not trained with proper privacy hygiene. High Trust Debt pushes toward Exact Unlearning regardless of sensitivity class.

  \item[Dimension 4: Legal Deadline.]
    GDPR Article 17 requires erasure ``without undue delay,'' interpreted in practice as 30 days. Full retraining of a large model may require weeks of compute. When deadline pressure and moderate sensitivity collide, Approximate Unlearning with a documented \textbf{Residual Risk Disclosure} to the data subject may be the legally defensible interim measure.
\end{description}
