\chapter{Efficient AI: Trustworthy Quantization \& Pruning}
\label{chap:efficient_ai}

\abstract{This chapter explores the critical intersection between trust and performance. While defenses like Differential Privacy and Secure Aggregation provide robust protection, they often impose significant computational and communication overhead. We analyze how to make these "heavy" defenses practical through model compression, quantization, and pruning. By moving inference to the edge and reducing the multi-party communication burden, efficient AI enables the large-scale adoption of trustworthy technologies in resource-constrained environments like ASEAN.}

\section{Privacy Requires Efficiency}
\label{sec:privacy_efficiency}

As we have seen in the preceding parts of this book, building a trustworthy system is computationally expensive. Differential Privacy requires per-sample gradient clipping and noise injection; Secure Aggregation requires multiple cryptographic rounds and key exchanges; and robust models often require larger architectures to maintain accuracy under noise.

If these models are too heavy to run on the consumer devices (smartphones, IoT sensors) where data is actually generated, architects are forced to move processing to the cloud, re-introducing the privacy risks we seek to avoid. For the Trustworthy AI Architect, efficiency is the enabling pillar that makes decentralized privacy a reality.

\section{Core Efficiency Techniques}
\label{sec:efficiency_techniques}

To reduce the footprint of a model while maintaining its trustworthiness, we employ three primary technical maneuvers derived from foundational compression research \cite{han2015}:

\begin{description}
  \item[Quantization:] 
        Reducing the numerical precision of model weights and activations (e.g., from 32-bit floating point to 8-bit integers). This dramatically reduces memory usage and speeds up inference with minimal loss in accuracy.
        \begin{equation}
        x_{int} = \text{round}\left(\frac{x_{fp}}{\text{scale}} + \text{zero\_point}\right)
        \end{equation}
  \item[Pruning:]
        Identifying and removing redundant neurons or synaptic connections that contribute little to the model's final prediction. \textit{Magnitude pruning} is a common heuristic:
        \begin{equation}
        \text{mask}_i = \begin{cases} 1 & \text{if } |w_i| > \text{threshold} \\ 0 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}
        \end{equation}
  \item[Knowledge Distillation:]
        Training a smaller, "student" model to mimic the outputs and behavior of a larger, "teacher" model. This allows for the deployment of a lightweight architecture that retains much of the knowledge and robustness of a massive ensemble.
\end{description}

\section{Neural Architecture Search (NAS)}
\label{sec:nas}

For high-end edge deployments, architects can use \textbf{Neural Architecture Search (NAS)} to automatically discover the optimal model structure for a specific hardware target. The objective is to minimize validation loss while respecting a strict latency or memory constraint:
\begin{equation}
\min_{\text{arch}} L_{\text{val}}(\text{arch}, w^*) \quad \text{s.t. } \text{Latency}(\text{arch}) \le T_{\text{max}}
\end{equation}

\section{Sustainable AI: Excellence through Energy Efficiency}
\label{sec:sustainable_ai}

In the 2026 technical landscape, the role of the Trustworthy AI Architect has expanded to include environmental accountability. As we have seen, privacy-preserving defenses add significant computational overhead. To satisfy the mandate of \textbf{Sustainable AI} \cite{schwartz2020} (aligned with Vietnam's 2026 carbon neutrality strategy), we must move beyond pure accuracy toward energy-aware optimization.

The "Carbon-Aware Architect" optimizes models using two primary metrics:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Power Usage Effectiveness (PUE):} Ensuring that the infrastructure supporting the AI (data centers, edge clusters) is optimized for thermal and electrical efficiency.
    \item \textbf{FLOPs-per-Watt:} A specialized efficiency metric that measures the amount of intellectual work (Floating Point Operations) performed per unit of energy consumed.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Green-by-Design Lifecycle}
To build sustainable trust, architectures must prioritize \textbf{Green-by-Design} principles. This includes using \textit{Conditional Computation} (only activates specific neural pathways when needed) and \textit{Carbon-Intelligent Scheduling} (performing heavy training or batch inference during hours when the local grid's renewable energy supply is at its peak). By optimizing for the ``Watt'' as well as the ``Bit,'' the architect ensures that AI remains a tool for long-term societal benefit rather than an ecological burden.

\section{Measuring the Carbon Cost of Trust: The
         Trust-Sustainability Trade-off Matrix}

\begin{quote}
\textit{``Every ZK-proof is an argument made in watts.
  The Trustworthy AI Architect must know the price of every
  cryptographic promise---not just in milliseconds, but in
  millijoules and milligrams of CO\textsubscript{2}.''}
\end{quote}

The preceding sections established that the
\emph{Carbon-Aware Architect} optimises for FLOPs-per-Watt
alongside bits-per-second, and the Green-by-Design Lifecycle
introduced the operational patterns---Conditional Computation and
Carbon-Intelligent Scheduling---that reduce the energy footprint
of AI inference.

What neither section addressed is the specific, quantified energy
cost of each \emph{trust mechanism} in the Trustworthy AI Stack.
This gap matters because the mechanisms that provide the strongest
privacy and verifiability guarantees---Homomorphic Encryption (HE),
Zero-Knowledge proofs (ZK-ML), and Secure Multi-Party Computation
(MPC)---are also the most computationally intensive.
An architect who adds a ZK-proof layer without understanding its
energy envelope risks building a system that is cryptographically
sovereign but environmentally unsustainable---a contradiction of
the mandate established in Section~\ref{sec:digital_sovereignty}.

This section provides the quantitative tools to resolve that
contradiction: a formal \textbf{Trust-Carbon Budget} model, an
empirically grounded \textbf{Trust-Sustainability Trade-off Matrix},
and a decision framework for selecting the minimum-carbon trust
mechanism that satisfies a deployment's verifiability requirement.

% ------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{The Trust-Carbon Budget: A Formal Model}
\label{subsec:trust-carbon-budget}
% ------------------------------------------------------------------

We define the \textbf{Trust-Carbon Budget} $\mathcal{C}$ of an AI
deployment as the total carbon cost attributable to trust
mechanisms---the overhead above and beyond the carbon cost of
running the underlying model inference with no privacy or
verifiability guarantees.

\paragraph{Carbon Cost of a Single Inference.}
For a single model inference of duration $T_{\mathrm{inf}}$
seconds on hardware with thermal design power $P_{\mathrm{TDP}}$
watts, served from a data centre with Power Usage Effectiveness
$\mathrm{PUE}$ and grid carbon intensity $I_{\mathrm{grid}}$
gCO\textsubscript{2}eq per kWh, the baseline carbon cost is:

\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:baseline-carbon}
  C_{\mathrm{base}}
    = \frac{P_{\mathrm{TDP}} \cdot T_{\mathrm{inf}}
            \cdot \mathrm{PUE}
            \cdot I_{\mathrm{grid}}}{3{,}600{,}000}
    \quad [\text{gCO}_2\text{eq}]
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Trust Overhead Factor.}
Each trust mechanism $m$ introduces a multiplicative overhead
$\alpha_m \geq 1$ on inference time and an additive setup cost
$C_m^{\mathrm{setup}}$ incurred once per session or certificate
validity period.
The total carbon cost of a trust-protected inference is:

\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:trust-carbon}
  C_m
    = \alpha_m \cdot C_{\mathrm{base}}
    + C_m^{\mathrm{setup}}
    \quad [\text{gCO}_2\text{eq}]
\end{equation}

The \textbf{Trust-Carbon Overhead} $\Delta C_m$ is the additional
carbon attributable solely to the trust mechanism:

\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:trust-overhead}
  \Delta C_m
    = C_m - C_{\mathrm{base}}
    = (\alpha_m - 1)\cdot C_{\mathrm{base}}
    + C_m^{\mathrm{setup}}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Deployment-Scale Carbon Budget.}
For a deployment serving $N$ inferences per day over a
deployment lifetime of $D$ days, the total trust-attributable
carbon cost is:

\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:total-carbon}
  \mathcal{C}_m
    = N \cdot D \cdot \Delta C_m
    \quad [\text{kgCO}_2\text{eq}]
\end{equation}

The architect's objective is to select mechanism $m^*$ that
satisfies the deployment's minimum required \textbf{Trust Level}
$\mathcal{T}_{\min}$ while minimising $\mathcal{C}_m$:

\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:trust-optimisation}
  m^*
    = \arg\min_{m\,:\;\mathcal{T}(m)\,\geq\,\mathcal{T}_{\min}}
      \mathcal{C}_m
\end{equation}

where $\mathcal{T}(m)$ is the Trust Level score of mechanism $m$,
defined formally in Section~\ref{subsec:trust-level-scoring}.

% ------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Trust Level Scoring}
\label{subsec:trust-level-scoring}
% ------------------------------------------------------------------

To make Equation~\eqref{eq:trust-optimisation} operational, we
define a \textbf{Trust Level} $\mathcal{T}(m) \in \{1, 2, 3, 4, 5\}$
for each mechanism along four dimensions that correspond to the
four pillars of the Trustworthy AI Stack
(Table~\ref{tab:trust_stack_2026}):

\begin{description}
  \item[$\mathcal{T}_{\mathrm{sec}}$: Security Guarantee.]
    The mechanism's resistance to adversarial model extraction,
    gradient inversion, and Byzantine attacks.
    Scored 1 (heuristic) to 5 (formally proven).

  \item[$\mathcal{T}_{\mathrm{priv}}$: Privacy Guarantee.]
    The mechanism's protection against membership inference and
    re-identification.
    Scored 1 (no guarantee) to 5 ($(\varepsilon, \delta)$-DP with
    $\varepsilon \leq 1$).

  \item[$\mathcal{T}_{\mathrm{ver}}$: Verifiability Guarantee.]
    The mechanism's ability to produce a cryptographic or formal
    proof of correct execution.
    Scored 1 (no proof) to 5 (zk-SNARK with public verification
    key).

  \item[$\mathcal{T}_{\mathrm{aln}}$: Alignment Auditability.]
    The mechanism's support for third-party auditing of model
    behaviour without revealing sensitive weights.
    Scored 1 (opaque) to 5 (full ZK-audit log).
\end{description}

The composite Trust Level is the weighted average:

\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:trust-level}
  \mathcal{T}(m)
    = w_{\mathrm{sec}}\,\mathcal{T}_{\mathrm{sec}}(m)
    + w_{\mathrm{priv}}\,\mathcal{T}_{\mathrm{priv}}(m)
    + w_{\mathrm{ver}}\,\mathcal{T}_{\mathrm{ver}}(m)
    + w_{\mathrm{aln}}\,\mathcal{T}_{\mathrm{aln}}(m)
\end{equation}

where $w_{\mathrm{sec}} + w_{\mathrm{priv}} + w_{\mathrm{ver}}
+ w_{\mathrm{aln}} = 1$.
Weights are set by the architect to reflect the deployment's
regulatory profile.

profiles corresponding to common Vietnamese deployment contexts
under Decree~356 and the AI Law.

\paragraph{Key Observations from the Matrix.}
Four structural patterns emerge from
Table~\ref{tab:trust-sustainability-matrix} that should govern
every architect's mechanism selection:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Differential Privacy is the highest
    trust-per-watt mechanism.}
    With a Trust Level of 3.2 and an energy overhead of only
    $0.08\,\text{Wh}$ per 1k tokens, DP provides the best
    ratio of privacy guarantee to carbon cost of any mechanism
    in the stack.
    It should be the \emph{default} privacy layer for all
    deployments, with stronger mechanisms added only when the
    regulatory or threat profile requires it.

  \item \textbf{TEEs occupy the ``efficient trust'' sweet spot
    for confidential computing.}
    At $1.8\times$ overhead and a Trust Level of 3.5, a
    TEE-protected inference is the most carbon-efficient path
    to hardware-rooted confidentiality.
    For deployments where ZK-proof generation is required
    primarily for compliance auditing rather than per-query
    verification, a TEE-plus-audit-log architecture
    reduces carbon cost by over $90\%$ compared to per-inference
    ZK-proofs.

  \item \textbf{ZK-proof generation carries an 18$\times$ energy
    overhead and must be amortised.}
    The $13.6\,\text{Wh}$ overhead per 1k tokens is the
    largest single-mechanism cost in the stack after full HE.
    Proof amortisation---generating one proof for a batch of
    $B$ inferences rather than one per inference---reduces the
    per-inference overhead to $13.6 / B\,\text{Wh}$.
    At $B = 100$, the per-inference ZK overhead becomes
    $0.136\,\text{Wh}$, comparable to a TEE.

  \item \textbf{Full Homomorphic Encryption is currently
    ecologically indefensible at inference scale.}
    A $250\times$ overhead translating to $95.6\,\text{gCO}_2\text{eq}$
    per 1k tokens means that a deployment serving 1 million
    daily inferences would emit approximately
    $34.9\,\text{tCO}_2\text{eq}$ per year from trust mechanisms
    alone---equivalent to the annual emissions of four
    passenger vehicles.
    Full HE inference should be treated as a research
    capability until dedicated hardware accelerators reduce
    this overhead by at least two orders of magnitude.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Worked Example: Selecting a Trust Mechanism for a
            Vietnamese Rural Healthcare Triage System}
\label{subsec:carbon-worked-example}
% ------------------------------------------------------------------

We apply the framework to an autonomous healthcare triage system:
a 2026 deployment in a Vietnamese rural clinic that processes
5{,}000 patient triage queries per day over a 365-day year, hosted
on a regional Vietnamese cloud provider with
$\mathrm{PUE} = 1.45$ and grid carbon intensity
$I_{\mathrm{grid}} = 480\,\text{gCO}_2\text{eq/kWh}$.

\paragraph{Step 1: Determine $\mathcal{T}_{\min}$.}
Using the Healthcare weight profile from
Table~\ref{tab:weight-profiles}:
$w_{\mathrm{sec}} = 0.25$,
$w_{\mathrm{priv}} = 0.35$,
$w_{\mathrm{ver}} = 0.25$,
$w_{\mathrm{aln}} = 0.15$.
The deployment requires Decree~356 compliance (mandatory DP
for sensitive health data) and Ministry of Health ZK-verification
of model weights.
This yields $\mathcal{T}_{\min} = 4.0$.

\paragraph{Step 2: Identify the candidate mechanism set.}
From Table~\ref{tab:trust-sustainability-matrix}, mechanisms
with $\mathcal{T}(m) \geq 4.0$ are:
DP-FL ($\mathcal{T} = 4.1$),
SecAgg ($\mathcal{T} = 4.0$),
ZK-Proof ($\mathcal{T} = 4.8$),
and HE ($\mathcal{T} = 4.6$).

\paragraph{Step 3: Compute deployment-scale carbon costs.}
Using Equation~\eqref{eq:total-carbon} with
$N = 5{,}000$, $D = 365$, and adjusting for PUE:

\begin{align}
  \label{eq:worked-carbon}
  \mathcal{C}_{\mathrm{DP\text{-}FL}}
    &= 5{,}000 \times 365 \times 0.61
       \times \tfrac{1.45}{1.3} \;\text{gCO}_2\text{eq}
    \approx 1{,}243\;\text{kgCO}_2\text{eq/year} \notag \\
  \mathcal{C}_{\mathrm{ZK\text{-}proof}}
    &= 5{,}000 \times 365 \times 6.53
       \times \tfrac{1.45}{1.3} \;\text{gCO}_2\text{eq}
    \approx 13{,}305\;\text{kgCO}_2\text{eq/year} \notag \\
  \mathcal{C}_{\mathrm{ZK\text{-}amortised},B=100}
    &= 5{,}000 \times 365 \times \tfrac{6.53}{100}
       \times \tfrac{1.45}{1.3} \;\text{gCO}_2\text{eq}
    \approx 133\;\text{kgCO}_2\text{eq/year}
\end{align}

\paragraph{Step 4: Apply the Decision Framework.}
The triage system requires verifiability (ZK-proof for Ministry
of Health audit) but the clinic's carbon budget is constrained.
The Decision Framework (Figure~\ref{fig:trust-carbon-decision})
routes to \textbf{TEE + ZK-Audit Log}: TEE-protected inference
for all 5{,}000 daily queries, with ZK-proofs generated only
for the daily audit batch submitted to the Ministry of Health
(approximately 50 representative queries per day).

\begin{align}
  \mathcal{C}_{m^*}
    &= \underbrace{5{,}000 \times 365 \times 0.31
       \times \tfrac{1.45}{1.3}}_{\text{TEE inference}}
    + \underbrace{50 \times 365 \times 6.53
      \times \tfrac{1.45}{1.3}}_{\text{ZK audit batch}} \notag \\
    &\approx 632 + 133
    = 765\;\text{kgCO}_2\text{eq/year}
\end{align}

This represents a $94\%$ carbon reduction compared to
per-inference ZK-proofs while maintaining a composite
Trust Level of $\mathcal{T} = 4.1$---above the required
threshold of $4.0$.

\subsection{Implementation: The Carbon-Aware Trust Selector}
\label{subsec:carbon-aware-selector}
% ------------------------------------------------------------------

Listing~\ref{lst:carbon-trust-selector} provides a minimal
implementation of the Trust-Carbon Budget calculator, enabling
architects to programmatically evaluate mechanism trade-offs
during system design.

\begin{lstlisting}[
  style=pythonstyle,
  caption={Carbon-Aware Trust Mechanism Selector: computes
           deployment-scale CO2 costs for each trust mechanism
           and returns the optimal selection given a minimum
           Trust Level requirement.},
  label={lst:carbon-trust-selector}
]
from dataclasses import dataclass
from typing import Optional

@dataclass
class TrustMechanism:
    name:           str
    trust_level:    float    # Composite T(m), scored 1-5
    alpha:          float    # Inference-time overhead multiplier
    delta_energy_wh: float   # Energy overhead per 1k tokens (Wh)
    setup_cost_wh:  float    # One-time setup energy per session

# -- Trust Mechanism Registry (from Table 15.X) ----------------
MECHANISMS = [
    TrustMechanism("DP (inference noise)",  3.2,   1.1,   0.08,  0.00),
    TrustMechanism("TEE (SGX/TrustZone)",   3.5,   1.8,   0.64,  0.02),
    TrustMechanism("Federated Learning",    2.8,   2.1,   0.88,  0.00),
    TrustMechanism("DP-FL",                 4.1,   2.6,   1.28,  0.00),
    TrustMechanism("SecAgg (MPC)",          4.0,   3.5,   2.00,  0.05),
    TrustMechanism("ZK-Proof (per query)",  4.8,  18.0,  13.60,  0.10),
    TrustMechanism("HE (CKKS full)",        4.6, 250.0, 199.20,  0.50),
]

def carbon_cost_kg(mechanism, inferences_day, days,
                   pue=1.3, grid_intensity=480.0,
                   zk_batch_size=None):
    """
    Compute total trust-attributable CO2 in kgCO2eq.
    For ZK-Proof, zk_batch_size amortises proof generation.
    """
    delta_e = mechanism.delta_energy_wh
    if mechanism.name.startswith("ZK") and zk_batch_size:
        delta_e = delta_e / zk_batch_size

    # Wh -> kWh -> gCO2eq -> kgCO2eq
    overhead_per_inf = (delta_e * pue * grid_intensity) / 1_000_000
    total_kg = inferences_day * days * overhead_per_inf
    return round(total_kg, 2)

def select_mechanism(t_min, inferences_day, days,
                     pue=1.45, grid_intensity=480.0,
                     zk_batch_size=100):
    """
    Return the minimum-carbon mechanism satisfying T(m) >= t_min.
    Implements Eq. (15.6): m* = argmin C_m s.t. T(m) >= T_min.
    """
    candidates = [m for m in MECHANISMS
                  if m.trust_level >= t_min]
    if not candidates:
        raise ValueError(f"No mechanism meets T_min={t_min}")

    costs = {
        m: carbon_cost_kg(m, inferences_day, days,
                          pue, grid_intensity, zk_batch_size)
        for m in candidates
    }
    optimal = min(costs, key=costs.get)
    return optimal, costs[optimal]

# -- Example: Vietnamese rural healthcare triage system --------
if __name__ == "__main__":
    mechanism, cost = select_mechanism(
        t_min=4.0, inferences_day=5_000, days=365,
        pue=1.45, zk_batch_size=100,
    )
    print(f"Optimal mechanism : {mechanism.name}")
    print(f"Trust Level       : {mechanism.trust_level:.1f}")
    print(f"Annual CO2 cost   : {cost:.1f} kgCO2eq/year")
\end{lstlisting}

\subsection{Reporting: The Trust-Carbon Disclosure Standard}
\label{subsec:trust-carbon-disclosure}
% ------------------------------------------------------------------

Vietnam's AI Law (effective March 2026) and the EU AI Act both
require that high-risk AI systems disclose their resource
consumption as part of their Conformity Assessment documentation.
We propose that this disclosure be extended to include
\textbf{Trust-Carbon Accounting}: a standardised report that
separates the carbon cost of model inference from the carbon
cost of each trust mechanism layered above it.

A minimal Trust-Carbon Disclosure comprises four fields:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Baseline Inference Carbon}
    ($C_{\mathrm{base}}$, kgCO\textsubscript{2}eq/year):
    The carbon cost of unprotected model inference at the
    declared query volume and hardware configuration.

  \item \textbf{Trust Mechanism Stack}:
    A list of all active trust mechanisms with their individual
    $\Delta C_m$ contributions, enabling auditors to identify
    disproportionately costly layers.

  \item \textbf{Mitigation Measures Applied}:
    A description of any carbon reduction strategies deployed
    (proof amortisation, Carbon-Intelligent Scheduling, model
    quantisation), with their measured impact on $\Delta C_m$.

  \item \textbf{Residual Trust-Carbon Overhead}
    ($\mathcal{C}_{m^*}$, kgCO\textsubscript{2}eq/year):
    The net trust-attributable carbon after mitigations,
    to be reported alongside the system's Trust Level
    $\mathcal{T}(m^*)$ as a pair:
    $\langle \mathcal{T},\, \mathcal{C} \rangle$.
\end{enumerate}

This $\langle \mathcal{T},\, \mathcal{C} \rangle$ pair is the
\emph{Trustworthy AI Sustainability Signature} of the deployment.
It expresses, in two numbers, the fundamental trade-off this
chapter has sought to quantify: how much trust the system
provides, and at what carbon cost.

A system reporting $\langle 4.1,\, 765 \rangle$ (the triage
example above) can be compared directly to an alternative
deployment reporting $\langle 4.8,\, 13{,}305 \rangle$ (per-inference
ZK-proofs).
The first is 94\% more carbon-efficient while meeting the same
regulatory threshold.
This comparison is only possible when architects measure, report,
and optimise the carbon cost of trust as rigorously as they
measure accuracy, latency, and privacy budget.
